What he will most care about for THIS role

A Director of Enterprise Applications at a PE-accelerated govtech SaaS company is co-owning strategic architectural decisions about the application portfolio, not just operating it. His concerns mix the strategic and the operational, in this rough order of weight:

Strategic (these may surface FIRST in a 30-minute conversation with a senior architect)

  1. AI substrate strategy — what's your view on custom-build vs. Salesforce Agentforce vs. Snowflake Cortex vs. hybrid? He's likely co-owning this decision right now. He doesn't want a candidate without an architectural philosophy; he also doesn't want one whose philosophy ignores his Salesforce contract and three-year roadmap.
  2. Will this AI system integrate cleanly into the existing portfolio, or become a new sprawl vector? His deepest portfolio-stewardship question. He has spent years cleaning up SaaS sprawl; he doesn't want another vector.

Operational (his help-desk-up heritage means these matter to him intensely)

  1. Identity and access model. Which service accounts? What Okta scopes? Lifecycle management when sales reps leave?
  2. Change control for prompt and model changes. What's the notification path when the CRO Brief content shifts overnight because of a prompt update? Ticketed changes or ship-it-Sunday-night?

  4. Audit trail. Govtech-adjacent → SOC 2 / FedRAMP posture bleeds into internal practice. Can any AI artifact be reconstructed for an audit?

The seven most likely questions, in rough order of probability

The composite identity matters here. Because he's a strategic Director of Enterprise Applications co-owning the AI substrate decision, the build-vs-buy and integration-architecture questions are likely to come earlier, not last. Because he came up through Help Desk, the operational questions (service accounts, change control, audit trail) carry intense personal weight.

Question 1 — "Walk me through how you'd connect the AI workspace to our Salesforce data"

Your answer in shape:

Lead with the read pattern: a dedicated service account in Salesforce with read-only scope on the objects you actually need (Account, Opportunity, OpportunityHistory, OpportunityContactRole, Activity, Task) — not blanket admin. Auth through Okta if their setup supports it; otherwise OAuth with refresh tokens stored in a secrets manager.

Then the data flow: nightly or near-real-time pull into a warehouse layer (Snowflake / BigQuery — ask what they have). The AI workspace reads from the warehouse, not from Salesforce directly, to avoid governor limits and to make the auth model cleaner.

For writes back to Salesforce — if any — through a separate service account with narrowly-scoped write permissions and idempotency keys, never overwriting source-of-record fields like Stage or Amount. Signals get written as custom objects with provenance; recommended actions get written as Tasks.

The framing: "I want to be a guest in your Salesforce instance, not a renovator."

Question 2 — "How would you handle service accounts and Okta-enforced access?"

Your answer in shape:

Service accounts per integration, not per application. Salesforce gets one. Gong gets one. Outreach gets one. Each scoped to the minimum read access the AI actually needs.

If Okta supports it, the service accounts authenticate through Okta with proper lifecycle management — when a credential rotates, the AI system knows about it through the Okta tenant. If not, secrets in a managed vault with rotation cadence.

Naming convention you'd follow: something like svc-revenue-ai-salesforce-read so the ownership is obvious in any audit.

The framing: "Your IAM stack is the spine; I'd attach the AI through it, not around it."

Question 3 — "What's your operating model — who carries the pager when this breaks at 6 AM Monday?"

Your answer in shape:

A senior architect operating without a runbook is a liability. You'd build the runbook with his team, not in parallel.

Monitoring tied to whatever observability stack NEOGOV uses — likely Datadog or New Relic. Alert thresholds on the AI workspace's outputs (eval-score drift, cost anomalies, failed jobs), not just up/down.

For the first 90 days you'd carry the pager yourself to learn the failure modes. After that, transition to a shared on-call model with the data engineering or RevOps tech team — whoever is the natural home — with documented runbooks for the top 5 failure modes.

The framing: "The first 90 days I expect to be the operator. After that, the team and I decide together where it lives."

Question 4 — "How do you think about change control for prompt and model changes?"

Your answer in shape:

Prompts are code. They live in version control, with peer review and an automated test set (golden inputs with expected output properties) that runs in CI before merge.

Production deployment is staged: shadow mode (the new prompt runs alongside the old, output compared, neither shown to users) → small percentage of real traffic → full traffic. Rollback is one click because the previous version is still deployable.

For visible-output changes — CRO Brief content shifting because of a prompt update — change notifications go through whatever notification channel the application team uses. If they're on FreshService or ServiceNow, the prompt change is a ticketed change with stakeholder notification.

The framing: "I expect prompt and model changes to go through your change-notification process. I've seen what 'I shipped it Sunday night' costs at enterprise scale."

Question 5 — "What's the audit trail you'd build?"

Your answer in shape:

Every artifact (CRO Brief, Director Scorecard, Seller Action Sheet) carries a unique ID. That ID points back to the snapshot of input data, the version of every prompt used, the exact model version, every tool call the agent made, and the intermediate results.

Given an artifact ID, anyone — a SOC 2 auditor, a customer doing a FOIA review, a curious CRO — can reconstruct exactly what the AI saw and what it produced. Reproducibility is the foundation; everything else is built on it.

The framing: "Govtech audit trails matter. I'd build the lineage from raw Salesforce data through the AI output so any Brief can be reconstructed for an audit."

Question 6 — "How have you worked with internal IT and application admin teams in past roles?"

Your answer in shape:

This is an open-ended question testing tone and posture. Tell a concrete story. The Microsoft engagement is a good one — you partner with the ABM AI team, which lives alongside the broader Microsoft application stack. You don't ask for admin rights; you partner with whoever owns the system you need.

The framing: "The pattern I work in: I treat the application admin team as the system experts. I bring AI architectural judgment. The intersection is where we land good decisions — they tell me what's possible inside the stack constraints, I tell them what's possible inside the AI architecture constraints, and we build something that respects both."

Question 7 — "What's your view on build-vs-buy — custom or Salesforce Einstein / Agentforce / Snowflake Cortex?"

This one likely comes early, not late. As a Director of Enterprise Applications co-owning this decision right now, he's not asking out of curiosity — he's testing whether you can be a peer-partner in the decision he's currently making.

Your answer in shape:

It depends on what NEOGOV's Salesforce contract looks like and what the three-year roadmap is.

Custom on OpenAI or Anthropic gives maximum control and best observability but costs engineering-team-months. Salesforce Einstein / Agentforce gives faster time-to-value but locks you to Salesforce's pricing curve and AI roadmap. Snowflake Cortex sits in the middle.

The honest answer for NEOGOV right now: probably hybrid pragmatic. Ingestion and signal aggregation close to the data layer (Snowflake or Databricks). Agent orchestration as a thin custom layer over a mature framework for multi-model flexibility. MCP-style tool exposure so neither layer is locked in. That gives you provider portability without giving up provider-specific features where they materially help.

The framing — and this matters because he's making this decision: "I have opinions on each direction, but I'd expect to be a working partner with you on the substrate selection, not the sole architect of it. The right answer depends on what your Salesforce footprint supports, what your three-year AI roadmap looks like, and what NEOGOV's risk posture on platform lock-in is. I'd want to learn those constraints from you before I'd commit to a direction."

Notice the peer-partnership signal: "a working partner with you," "learn those constraints from you." He's making the decision; you can be in the conversation as a senior peer, not as a junior implementer.

Closer — questions to ask him

Have 2-3 ready. He'll likely give you 3-5 minutes at the end.

  1. "What does the integration map look like today between Salesforce, NetSuite, and the rest of the application stack? Where are the brittle seams?"
  2. "What's your ITSM tool of choice at NEOGOV — FreshService, ServiceNow, something else?" (His native question.)
  3. "How does IT and RevOps work together when there's a new system to deploy — what's the partnership pattern?"
  4. "What would success look like at six months for this role from your seat — not from Nick's?"

The fourth one is the strongest. It invites him to articulate his lens explicitly and gives you actionable signal for if/when there's an offer to navigate.

What NOT to do

  • Don't lead with AI architecture. Lead with integration architecture and operating model. The AI is the inner layer; the application stack is the outer shell. Zachary cares about the shell.
  • Don't critique the existing stack. Even if you think FreshService is the wrong choice or Salesforce CPQ is messy. Zachary owns this stack. Criticizing it on a first call is the cowboy move.
  • Don't claim deep Okta / NetSuite / RightRev expertise you haven't built. You've worked alongside identity layers and financial systems; you haven't administered them. "Familiar paradigm, would want to ramp on your specific tenancy" is the honest and confident answer.
  • Don't pile credentials. Zachary has your resume. The HP-era patent count and the Ph.D. will not move him; the help-desk-up arc means he's a wary consumer of senior-architect resumes. Stay grounded in the concrete: integration patterns, auth models, runbooks, change control.
  • Don't ask about salary, remote work, or side consulting. All three belong to later rounds. This is the IT-fit lens; surfacing comp or scheduling reads as off-target.
  • Don't surface Nick's overqualified concern. That was Nick's worry. Don't pre-defend against a question Zachary isn't asking. Zachary's concern is operational respect, not flight risk.
  • Don't telegraph the Unify timeline. Same rule as Nick.
  • Don't bring up Integrating Consulting. Same rule. Offer-stage conversation.
  • Don't volunteer opinions on rebuilding things. If asked, give a measured answer that leads with "I'd want to understand the existing stack first." Reserve build-or-buy opinions for the second or third question on a topic, not the first.

Closing posture

Thirty minutes is focused. Three things to demonstrate:

  1. You think in integration architecture, not just AI architecture — your AI system lives inside his application portfolio, not next to it.
  2. You respect IT processes — change control, ticketing, audit trails, SOC 2 posture, service-account hygiene. Senior judgment expresses itself as respect, not as opinions.
  3. You're a partner to his team, not a competitor for it — the Salesforce admin, the help desk, the IAM operator are people you'll collaborate with daily, not work around.

Anushka shortlisted you. Nick green-lit the sales-motion fit. Zachary is the last functional gate before the offer conversation. The shape of this call is narrower than Nick's was — focused probing, not strategic discussion — but the bar is just as load-bearing.

Walk in calm. Speak in his idiom. Let him stay the senior in the application-stack conversation. Trust that the AI architecture seniority lands without being asserted.
