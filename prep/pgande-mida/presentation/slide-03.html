The Hard Problem — The Streak Test

  • Streak defects: perceivable by human vision; affected customer quality perception
  • Source of the majority of our RMAs (Returned Merchandise Authorizations)
  • The catch — the test was MANUALLY TUNED:
    • 2+ man-weeks to tune per product (standard)
  • One product I tuned for 2+ months; the main architect backed the time
  • Team members were specifically evaluated on streak-detection skill vs. other engineers
  • Reality: inspection fatigue is real — visual grading degrades over hours

Two-plus man-weeks per product to manually tune — and inspection fatigue is real.

The Streak Test was the test that caught a specific defect class no other test could catch — defects that human eyes would see when the product reached the customer, which is why streaks drove the majority of our RMAs. The catch — and this is where it gets interesting — was that the Streak Test was manually tuned. Two-plus man-weeks per product, standard. I once spent over two months tuning it for a particularly challenging product, and the main architect backed my need to take that time.

Members of our group were specifically evaluated on our ability to find these defects compared to other engineers, because we did this work regularly and they didn't. And the inescapable reality was that inspection fatigue is real — visual grading degrades over hours, and we had to schedule breaks deliberately to guard against it.

Q&A CUE (the gold question): "Does this kind of human-perceivable but algorithmically-hard-to-define defect ring familiar with what MIDA is facing?"
